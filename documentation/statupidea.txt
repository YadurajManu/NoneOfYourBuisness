01 Platform Philosophy & Vision
Why MediLifecycle Exists and What Problem It Solves
The Problem We Solve

Healthcare delivery has long suffered from a fundamental structural failure: the people who matter

most in a patient's care journey — the patient, their family, their primary doctor, their specialists, and
the clinical support team — all work in different systems, receive different information, and often

discover critical updates too late or not at all. A specialist may not know the patient's GP changed a

medication last week. A family member waits in a corridor with no information for six hours. A
patient is handed a discharge summary written in language they cannot understand and is expected

to self-manage a complex chronic condition.

MediLifecycle was built to solve this problem at its root. It does not add another point solution to an

already fragmented ecosystem. It replaces fragmentation with a single, living, continuously updated
platform that every participant in the care journey accesses — according to their role, their

permissions, and their needs.

!
Core Insight
The most dangerous moments in healthcare are transitions — when a patient moves from ED to
ward, from hospital to home, from primary care to specialist. MediLifecycle eliminates information
loss at every transition by keeping all stakeholders on a single, shared, real-time record.
Design Principles

Every feature and workflow in MediLifecycle is governed by five inviolable design principles that
your team should understand deeply before deploying the platform.

What MediLifecycle Is Not

To position this correctly with your hospital or clinic leadership, it is equally important to be clear
about what MediLifecycle is not.

It is not an EHR replacement. MediLifecycle integrates with your existing EHR (Epic, Cerner,
Meditech, eClinicalWorks, and others) via HL7 FHIR. It adds a coordination, communication,
and AI intelligence layer on top of your existing clinical data infrastructure.
It is not a telemedicine platform. While it supports secure messaging and communication
between all parties, it does not replace video consultation platforms. It complements them.
It is not a billing system. It generates billing-relevant data and flags discrepancies, but it
does not replace your practice management or revenue cycle management systems.
It is not a standalone AI diagnostic tool. The AI provides clinical decision support and
documentation assistance, but all clinical decisions remain the responsibility of the licensed
physician.

Architecture: The Five User Experiences

MediLifecycle is a single platform with five distinct front-end experiences, each designed from the

ground up for its user's context, technical literacy, emotional state, and information needs. A nurse
practitioner and a patient's spouse should never be looking at the same interface — and with

MediLifecycle, they never are.

The Complete 10-Stage Patient Lifecycle
Every Stage Explained in Full — Triggers, Actions, AI Role, Metrics
The MediLifecycle platform organises every patient's care journey into ten discrete lifecycle stages.

These stages are not rigid silos — a patient can move forward, pause, or return to a prior stage as
their clinical situation demands. What matters is that at every point in time, every authorised user

knows exactly which stage the patient is in, what has happened so far, and what comes next. The

platform enforces consistency while allowing clinical flexibility.

!
Stage Transitions
Only the Primary Doctor (or a delegated clinical lead) can advance or reverse a patient's lifecycle
stage. All stage transitions are timestamped, attributed to the authorising clinician, and recorded
in the permanent audit trail. Family members and patients receive an AI-generated notification
whenever the stage changes.
01

Patient Registration & Profile Creation
The patient's journey begins when they are registered on the MediLifecycle platform.
Registration can be initiated by admin staff (for in-person admissions), by the patient
themselves (for elective or planned care pathways), or automatically via HL7 FHIR
integration when a new patient record is created in the connected EHR. A complete digital
health profile is created, including demographic information, insurance and payer details,
emergency contact and family access preferences, baseline health information, and consent
records.
Who Acts: Admin Staff, Patient (self-registration), EHR Integration (automated)
AI Engine: Auto-populates all fields for which data exists in the connected EHR, flagging any
mandatory fields that are incomplete. Verifies insurance eligibility in real time against the payer
database. Detects and merges duplicate records. Generates the patient's first AI onboarding
message, welcoming them to the platform and explaining their rights and capabilities.
Stage Outcomes: Verified patient profile created; health file opened; patient and primary doctor
notified; family access preferences recorded; baseline AI risk score generated.
Key Metrics: Registration completion rate, time-to-registration, duplicate record detection rate,
insurance verification pass rate.
02

Initial Clinical Assessment
The primary doctor conducts the first clinical encounter — whether in person, via telehealth,
or via review of a referred patient's records. Clinical notes, presenting complaint, initial
diagnoses (ICD-10 coded), vital signs, and assessment findings are entered into the record.
The patient is asked to upload any prior medical records, test results, specialist letters, or
home monitoring data they hold. Medication reconciliation is completed and the patient's
current medication list is confirmed.
Who Acts: Primary Doctor (clinical entry), Patient (document upload, history confirmation)
AI Engine: Suggests ICD-10 diagnostic codes based on free-text clinical notes. Performs
real-time medication reconciliation, flagging any duplicates, contraindications, or gaps. Generates
an automatic care gap analysis, highlighting any screening or preventive care that is overdue
based on age, diagnosis, and clinical guidelines. Summarises any prior records the patient has
uploaded and extracts key data points.
Stage Outcomes: Baseline clinical record established; problem list created; medication list
confirmed; care gaps identified; initial AI risk scores generated for deterioration, readmission, and
medication non-adherence.
Key Metrics: Assessment completion time, care gap identification rate, medication reconciliation
accuracy, ICD-10 code suggestion acceptance rate.
03

Diagnostic Investigation & Record Building
Following initial assessment, diagnostic investigations are ordered: laboratory tests, imaging,
specialist referrals, ECGs, pulmonary function tests, or any other investigation relevant to the
clinical picture. Results are returned directly into the patient's record. The patient and all
authorised care team members can contribute documents to the record continuously. The
record grows in real time as results arrive.
Who Acts: Primary Doctor (orders), Patient (uploads, home test results), Lab/Imaging Systems
(automated result delivery), Specialist (if already engaged)
AI Engine: Flags out-of-range laboratory results immediately upon receipt, with severity grading
(informational, clinically significant, critical). Detects when an ordered investigation result is
overdue and generates a chase notification. Classifies and indexes every incoming document.
Suggests follow-up investigations based on the pattern of results. Generates a plain-language
explanation of each result for the patient's view.
Stage Outcomes: Complete investigation results received and indexed; critical values actioned;
patient informed of results in accessible language; follow-up investigations identified.
Key Metrics: Result turnaround time, critical value notification time (target under 4 minutes),
investigation completion rate, document classification accuracy.
04

Diagnosis Confirmation & Care Plan Creation
Based on completed investigations and clinical assessment, the primary doctor confirms the
diagnosis and creates a formal care plan. The care plan includes the diagnosis, treatment
objectives, treatment modalities (medications, procedures, lifestyle interventions, referrals),
monitoring schedule, patient education requirements, and discharge/transition criteria. The
care plan is communicated to the patient in plain language and to family members as
appropriate.
Who Acts: Primary Doctor (diagnosis and care plan creation), Patient (care plan review and
consent), Specialist (if already engaged — input to care plan)
AI Engine: Drafts a structured care plan document based on the confirmed diagnosis and clinical
guidelines, populated with patient-specific data. Checks the proposed care plan against clinical
guidelines for the confirmed diagnosis and flags any deviations for physician review. Generates a
plain-language version of the care plan for the patient. Creates a monitoring schedule and adds all
follow-up items to the care team's task list. Identifies any clinical trials or evidence-based
pathways relevant to the diagnosis.
Stage Outcomes: Confirmed diagnosis recorded; care plan agreed and communicated; patient
consent documented; monitoring schedule active; family notifications sent.
Key Metrics: Care plan creation time, patient care plan comprehension score (measured via
post-explanation quiz), guideline adherence rate, consent documentation rate.
05

Specialist Referral & Multi-Disciplinary Team Engagement
Where the care plan requires specialist input, the primary doctor initiates a referral through
the platform. The specialist is invited to join the care team, receives an AI-generated
specialty-specific case summary, and adds their expertise to the shared record. For complex
cases, multiple specialists may be engaged simultaneously. All specialist contributions are
visible to the full care team in real time, preventing the information siloes that characterise
traditional referral processes.
Who Acts: Primary Doctor (referral initiation), Specialist (case review and consultation), Patient
(notified of care team additions)
AI Engine: Generates the referral letter, pre-populated with all clinically relevant information from
the patient's record — the physician reviews and approves rather than drafting from scratch.
Matches the referral to the most appropriate available specialist based on specialty, patient
location, payer network, and urgency score. Notifies the specialist immediately upon referral.
Generates a specialty-specific case summary for the specialist on first access. Monitors for
specialist response and escalates if no acknowledgement is received within the defined SLA.
Stage Outcomes: Specialist engaged and acknowledged; specialty-specific case summary
delivered; specialist's care plan contribution received; cross-specialty interaction checks
completed.
Key Metrics: Referral letter generation time, specialist acknowledgement time (SLA: 4 hours
urgent, 24 hours routine), multi-disciplinary input completeness rate.
06

Active Treatment Delivery
Treatment is actively underway — medications are being administered, procedures are being
performed, or a structured therapy programme is in progress. This is often the longest stage,
spanning days to months for complex cases. The platform continuously monitors adherence
to the care plan, flags deviations, tracks the patient's reported symptoms and wellbeing, and
keeps all stakeholders informed of progress. Family members receive regular AI-generated
updates.
Who Acts: Primary Doctor (treatment oversight and orders), Specialist (procedure delivery,
specialist treatment), Patient (medication adherence, symptom reporting), Nursing/Clinical Staff
(care delivery documentation)
AI Engine: Monitors medication adherence signals and flags when the patient has not confirmed
a scheduled dose. Tracks vital signs and patient-reported outcomes against expected treatment
response curves, alerting the care team if the patient is not responding as expected. Sends the
patient daily contextual check-ins calibrated to their current treatment phase. Automatically pushes
family updates at key treatment milestones. Detects early warning signs of adverse drug reactions
from symptom reports.
Stage Outcomes: Treatment adherence documented; patient response tracked; adverse events
identified and actioned; family informed at milestones; care plan updated based on treatment
response.
Key Metrics: Medication adherence rate, patient-reported outcome completion rate, adverse
event detection time, care plan deviation alert response time.
07

Inpatient Monitoring & Ward Management
For patients who are admitted to hospital, this stage covers the full inpatient period. Clinical
observations, nursing notes, ward round summaries, medication administration records, fluid
balance charts, and procedure records are all captured in the platform. The care team can
see the complete inpatient picture in real time. Family members receive updates tied to key
inpatient events.
Who Acts: Primary Doctor (ward rounds, clinical decisions), Specialist (consult rounds), Nursing
Staff (observation entry, medication administration), Patient (symptom reporting via bedside tablet
or mobile)
AI Engine: Aggregates all inpatient observations and flags deterioration based on validated early
warning score models (NEWS2 or equivalent). Generates ward round summary drafts for
physician review. Alerts the care team when NEWS2 score crosses defined thresholds. Tracks
fluid balance and flags imbalances. Notifies family when significant inpatient events occur (e.g.,
transfer to ICU, return from surgery, change in clinical status).
Stage Outcomes: Complete inpatient record maintained; deterioration detected and actioned
early; care team informed in real time; family kept updated through admission.
Key Metrics: Early warning score documentation compliance, deterioration alert response time,
family notification delivery rate, nursing documentation completeness.
08

Procedure Management
This stage covers any discrete clinical procedure — from minor outpatient procedures to
major surgery — including pre-operative assessment, the procedure itself, and the immediate
post-operative or recovery period. The platform coordinates the procedural team, manages
consent documentation, tracks the procedure in real time, and delivers automated family
updates at key procedural milestones.
Who Acts: Primary Doctor (procedure authorisation), Specialist/Surgeon (procedure delivery),
Anaesthetics (pre-op assessment documentation), Patient (consent, pre-op instructions), Family
(notification recipients)
AI Engine: Generates and tracks consent documentation, flagging if any required consent is
missing before the procedure can be marked as commenced. Pushes automated real-time
updates to family at three milestones: procedure commenced, procedure completed, patient in
recovery. Drafts the post-procedure note for physician completion. Monitors post-operative vital
signs for early detection of complications. Generates the patient's plain-language explanation of
what happened and what to expect in recovery.
Stage Outcomes: Pre-operative checklist complete; consent documented; family informed at all
procedural milestones; post-procedure note completed; recovery monitoring active.
Key Metrics: Consent documentation rate, family notification delivery time at each milestone,
post-procedure note completion time, post-operative complication detection rate.
09

Discharge Planning & Transition of Care
As the patient approaches the end of their acute care episode, discharge planning begins.
This is one of the highest-risk stages for adverse outcomes — information loss, medication
errors, missed follow-up appointments, and inadequate family preparation are all common
causes of avoidable readmission. MediLifecycle addresses each of these risks explicitly. The
AI drafts the discharge summary, reconciles medications, generates the patient's
plain-language home care instructions, and ensures every follow-up item is scheduled and
tracked.
Who Acts: Primary Doctor (discharge decision and summary sign-off), Specialist
(specialty-specific discharge instructions), Admin Staff (appointment scheduling, transport),
Patient (discharge instruction review and acknowledgement), Family (preparation briefing)
AI Engine: Drafts the complete discharge summary in 60 seconds, pre-populated with the full
episode of care, confirmed diagnoses, procedures performed, medication changes, and follow-up
requirements — physician reviews and approves. Performs final medication reconciliation and
generates a patient-friendly medication guide. Schedules all follow-up appointments and adds
reminders to the patient's and care team's calendars. Generates a plain-language home care
instruction sheet for the patient. Creates a family briefing document covering what to expect at
home, red-flag symptoms to watch for, and how to access help.
Stage Outcomes: Discharge summary completed and distributed; medications reconciled; all
follow-up appointments scheduled; patient and family briefed; post-discharge monitoring activated.
Key Metrics: Discharge summary completion time, medication reconciliation accuracy, follow-up
appointment scheduling rate (target: 100% before discharge), readmission rate at 7 and 30 days.
10

Post-Discharge Monitoring & Lifecycle Closure
The care journey does not end at discharge. The post-discharge period is when patients are
most vulnerable to deterioration, medication errors, and care plan non-adherence.
MediLifecycle maintains active monitoring through this phase — checking in with the patient
regularly, analysing their symptom reports, tracking medication adherence, and alerting the
care team if any signal suggests the patient is at risk. The lifecycle is only formally closed
when all follow-up requirements have been met, the patient is stable, and the primary doctor
confirms closure.

Who Acts: Primary Doctor (monitoring oversight and closure authorisation), Patient (daily
check-ins, symptom reporting, medication confirmation), Family (active support and
communication)

AI Engine: Delivers daily or weekly AI check-ins to the patient (frequency configured by the care
team based on clinical risk), structured around the patient's specific care plan and recovery
milestones. Analyses patient responses for deterioration signals and alerts the care team
immediately if concerning patterns emerge. Tracks follow-up appointment attendance and chases
the patient and care team if appointments are missed. Generates a readmission risk score that
updates in real time based on patient-reported data. Produces the final lifecycle closure report,
including a complete clinical summary, outcome documentation, and patient satisfaction score.

Stage Outcomes: All follow-up appointments completed; patient confirmed as stable;
outstanding care items resolved; lifecycle closure authorised; final health summary generated and
delivered to patient.

Key Metrics: Post-discharge check-in completion rate, deterioration alert response time,
follow-up appointment attendance rate, 30-day readmission rate, patient lifecycle closure
satisfaction score.

Document Intelligence & Upload Management
Every File, Classified, Indexed, and Actioned in Under 90 Seconds
One of the most significant sources of clinical risk in healthcare is documents that arrive and are not

seen — or are seen by the wrong person, too late. A radiology report that lands in an unmonitored
inbox. A specialist letter that is filed but never linked to the patient's active care plan. A home blood

glucose reading that the patient brings to an appointment but has no way to contribute to their

formal record. MediLifecycle's Document Intelligence Engine eliminates all of these failure modes.

4.1 How Document Intelligence Works

Every document that enters the MediLifecycle ecosystem — whether uploaded manually, pushed

from an integrated lab or imaging system, or imported via HL7 FHIR — passes through a five-step
AI processing pipeline before it is presented to any user.

Processing Step What Happens
Step 1: Classification The AI identifies what type of document it is: lab result, radiology
report, prescription, specialist letter, consent form, insurance
document, home monitoring data, prior medical history, discharge
summary, referral letter, or a custom category defined by the
institution. Classification accuracy across the standard document
library exceeds 97%.
Step 2: Data Extraction Structured data is extracted from the document: for a lab result, this
means test name, value, reference range, collection date, and
reporting lab. For a radiology report, this means modality, body region,
key findings, and radiologist recommendation. For a prescription, this
means drug name, dose, frequency, prescriber, and dispensing
instructions.
Step 3: Clinical Flagging Extracted values are assessed against clinical thresholds.
Out-of-range lab values are graded by severity (informational, clinically
significant, critical). Radiology findings that use terms suggesting
malignancy, haemorrhage, or fracture are flagged regardless of the
radiologist's overall assessment. Drug names are cross-referenced
against the patient's current medication list for interactions.
Step 4: Record Linking The document is linked to the relevant encounter, care plan item,
specialist referral, or order. A lab result ordered on a specific date is
automatically linked to the originating order, closing the loop and
removing it from the outstanding results list.
Step 5: Routing &
Notification
Based on the document type and any clinical flags, the AI routes
notifications to the appropriate users. A critical lab result triggers an
immediate alert to the primary doctor and, if outside hours, the on-call
physician. A specialist letter triggers a review request to the primary
doctor. A patient-uploaded document is flagged for physician
acknowledgement.

5.2 AI Capabilities: Primary Doctor

The primary doctor's AI interface is the most clinically deep and technically demanding. It is

Clinical Query & Decision Support

Natural language clinical queries: 'What is her creatinine trend over the last 18 months?' —

Differential diagnosis support: 'She has fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, and

Specialty-Scoped Intelligence

5.6 AI Safeguards & Governance

!
Critical Safeguard
The AI cannot take any clinical action independently. It cannot place orders, prescribe
medications, change a patient's care plan, or send clinical information to patients without explicit
physician approval. Every AI-generated clinical document is a draft that requires physician review
and authorisation before it reaches any recipient.
Safeguard How It Works

Source Attribution Every AI assertion drawn from the patient's record is accompanied by
a source reference: the document name, upload date, and the specific
data point cited. Physicians can click through to the source document
at any time. The AI never makes unattributable clinical statements.

Confidence Scoring All AI diagnostic suggestions, risk scores, and clinical
recommendations are presented with an explicit confidence score and
the evidence base underlying that score. Low-confidence suggestions
are clearly labelled and the physician is prompted to weigh them with
additional clinical judgment.

Physician Approval Gates Defined categories of AI output require physician approval before
delivery: all documents sent to patients, all prior authorisation
submissions, all referral letters, all discharge summaries. The approval
workflow is built into the interface — physicians cannot accidentally
bypass it.

Immutable Audit Trail Every AI query, AI response, AI alert, and AI-drafted document is
logged in an immutable audit trail. The log records: the query, the
context used, the response generated, the user who reviewed it, any
edits made, and the final disposition (approved, rejected, modified).
Retained for 7 years.

Hallucination Prevention The AI is architecturally constrained to draw only from the patient's
actual record when making clinical statements. It is not permitted to
extrapolate from general medical knowledge to fill gaps in a patient's
record — it explicitly flags what it knows, what it does not know, and
what it needs more information about.

Human Override Always
Available

Any AI recommendation, alert, or risk score can be overridden by the
responsible clinician. The override is recorded in the audit trail with the
clinician's stated reason. Override patterns are reviewed in the
institutional analytics dashboard to identify systematic AI calibration
issues.

7.2 The Medication Management Workflow

Medication errors are among the most common and most preventable sources of patient harm.

MediLifecycle's medication management workflow is designed to make errors structurally difficult at
every stage of the medication lifecycle.

Stage What Happens
1. Prescription Entry Physician enters drug name (auto-complete), dose, frequency, route,
duration, and indication. AI simultaneously checks: drug-drug interactions,
drug-allergy interactions, renal/hepatic dose adjustment requirements,
controlled substance schedule compliance, and formulary status.
2. AI Review & Flags Any identified risks are presented to the prescriber with severity grading
before the prescription can be submitted. Green: no issues. Amber:
potential interaction — review required. Red: contraindicated — requires
documented override with clinical justification.
3. Formulary & PA
Determination

AI checks the patient's payer formulary in real time. If the prescribed drug
is non-formulary or requires prior authorisation, AI automatically initiates
the PA process and presents the physician with alternative formulary
options ranked by clinical equivalence.
4. Prescription
Transmission

Approved prescription is transmitted electronically to the patient's selected
pharmacy. Controlled substances comply with state-specific electronic
prescribing requirements. Specialty pharmacy prescriptions include any
required manufacturer support enrolment documentation.
5. Patient Medication
Guide

AI generates a patient-facing medication guide: drug name (brand and
generic), what it is for, how to take it, what to do if a dose is missed, side
effects to watch for, foods or activities to avoid, and when to seek help.
Sent to the patient's app.
6. Adherence
Monitoring

AI sends daily medication reminders calibrated to the prescribed schedule.
Patient confirms doses taken via a one-tap interface. Non-adherence after
two consecutive missed confirmations triggers a care team alert.
Adherence data is graphed in the clinical dashboard.
7. Refill Management AI monitors prescription days-supply and initiates refill alerts 7 days before
the expected run-out date. If the patient has not filled the refill within 3 days
of expected run-out, AI alerts the prescribing physician and the patient
simultaneously.
8. Discontinuation &
Reconciliation

When a medication is discontinued, the record is updated, the patient is
notified with an explanation, and the medication is moved to the 'inactive'
list with a documented discontinuation reason. All medications are
reconciled at every stage transition.
7.3 Prior Authorisation (PA) Automation

Prior authorisation is one of the most administratively burdensome processes in US healthcare —

and one of the most common causes of treatment delay. MediLifecycle's PA automation module

reduces average PA submission time from 22 minutes to under 4 minutes and improves first-pass
approval rates from a typical 67% to 89%.

PA Requirement Detection: AI checks every order against the patient's payer's real-time
formulary and PA requirements database — updated nightly from payer feeds — before the
physician submits.
Automated PA Request Assembly: AI gathers all required documentation automatically:
medical necessity justification, supporting diagnosis codes, relevant clinical history, prior
treatment history demonstrating step therapy compliance, and prescribing physician
credentials.
Real-Time PA Submission: PA request is submitted to the payer electronically via X12 278
transaction or payer API. Status is tracked in real time and displayed on the physician's
dashboard.
Peer-to-Peer Request Management: if the PA is denied, AI drafts a peer-to-peer review
request and a clinical appeal letter, pre-populated with the patient's specific clinical
evidence, for physician review and submission.
PA Status Tracking: all open PA requests are visible on the care team dashboard with
current status, payer response deadline, and next action required.

8.3 Infrastructure & Hosting

Infrastructure Element Specification
Cloud Infrastructure MediLifecycle is hosted on Amazon Web Services (AWS) US-East and
US-West regions (configurable for international deployments). All
compute, storage, and networking resources are HIPAA-eligible AWS
services. We do not share infrastructure with other SaaS tenants —
each hospital or health system partner runs in a dedicated, logically
isolated environment.
Data Residency All patient data is stored in US-based AWS data centres by default.
International partners may select EU, APAC, or other regional data
centres to meet local data residency requirements. Data never leaves
the configured residency boundary.
Uptime Architecture Active-active multi-availability-zone deployment with automatic
failover. No single point of failure. 99.9% uptime SLA. Planned
maintenance is conducted in the configured off-peak window (default
02:00-04:00 local time) with 7-day advance notice.
Disaster Recovery Recovery Point Objective (RPO): 1 hour (maximum data loss in
disaster scenario). Recovery Time Objective (RTO): 4 hours
(maximum time to full service restoration). Full DR failover testing
conducted quarterly.
Performance API response time SLA: p95 under 200ms for standard queries. AI
document generation: under 60 seconds for standard documents.
Document upload processing pipeline: under 90 seconds from upload
to fully indexed and accessible.
Scalability Horizontally scalable. No hard limits on patient records, document
volumes, or concurrent users. Institutions with 10,000+ active patients
are supported under the Enterprise tier with dedicated capacity
planning.
8.4 Integration Implementation Guide

The following steps outline the standard integration implementation process for a new hospital or
clinic partner.

Pre-Integration Discovery (Day 1-2): MediLifecycle's Integration Architect meets with your IT
team to inventory all source systems, document existing data flows, identify integration
methods for each system, and produce a detailed integration specification.
Credentials & Connectivity (Day 3-4): API credentials, OAuth client IDs, and network

for all payers in the institution's payer mix.
End-to-End Testing (Day 13-14): Full end-to-end scenario testing with representative patient
data. All critical paths validated: registration, results routing, AI alert delivery, document
processing, notification delivery.
Go-Live & Hypercare (Day 15): Go-live. Integration Architect remains on standby for the first
two weeks post-launch for rapid issue resolution.

9.2 Technical Security Controls

Security Control Implementation Detail
Encryption at Rest AES-256 encryption for all stored data. Database-level encryption
enforced at the storage layer. Encryption keys managed in AWS KMS
with automatic annual rotation. Customer-managed keys (CMK)
available for Enterprise tier partners who require independent key
custody.
Encryption in Transit TLS 1.3 enforced for all data in transit. TLS 1.2 as minimum fallback.
Certificate pinning on mobile applications. No unencrypted data channels
permitted anywhere in the platform.
Authentication Multi-factor authentication (MFA) mandatory for all users. Supported
MFA methods: TOTP authenticator app, SMS OTP, hardware security
key (FIDO2/WebAuthn). Single sign-on (SSO) via SAML 2.0 or OIDC for
institutional identity provider integration.
Authorisation & Access
Control
Role-based access control (RBAC) enforced at every API call.
Fine-grained permission system: access is defined at the patient level,
not just the role level. Attribute-based access control (ABAC) for
specialty-specific data filtering. No access is permitted beyond what is
explicitly granted.
Network Security Web Application Firewall (WAF) in front of all public-facing endpoints.
DDoS protection via AWS Shield Advanced. Network segmentation:
application, data, and management planes are fully isolated. All
inter-service communications are encrypted and authenticated via
mTLS.
Vulnerability
Management
Automated dependency scanning on every code deployment. Static
application security testing (SAST) and dynamic application security
testing (DAST) in CI/CD pipeline. Third-party penetration testing
quarterly. Bug bounty programme for responsible disclosure.
Audit Logging Immutable audit log of every user action, API call, data access, AI query,
and AI output. Logs are written to tamper-proof storage (AWS CloudTrail
+ S3 with Object Lock). Log retention: 7 years. Logs are available to
institution compliance teams via the Audit Export API.
Data Loss Prevention DLP controls prevent PHI from being exported via unapproved channels.
Copy/paste of PHI outside the application is monitored. Bulk data
exports require elevated authorisation and are logged with business
justification.
9.3 Patient Data Rights

Right to Access: patients can download their complete health record, including all
documents, AI-generated summaries, and interaction logs, at any time from the patient
portal.
Right to Correct: patients can flag inaccurate information in their record; corrections are
reviewed and applied by the responsible clinician with full version history preserved.
Right to Restrict: patients can restrict the sharing of specific portions of their record with
specific users, including family members, at any time.
Right to Portability: patients can export their data in FHIR R4 format for import into another
health platform or personal health record.
Right to Erasure: patients can request deletion of personal data. Clinical records are subject
to legal retention requirements — where erasure is legally permissible, it is carried out within
30 days.
Consent Management: all consent decisions (data sharing, family access, research
participation) are recorded with timestamp, method of consent, and version of consent form.
Patients can review and modify consents at any time from the patient portal.

Frequently Asked Questions
Answers to the Questions Your Team Will Ask
Question Answer

Can patients see everything in
their record, including clinical
notes?

By default, clinical notes written by physicians are not
automatically visible to patients. The primary doctor controls
which elements of the clinical record are marked as
'patient-visible'. AI-generated plain-language summaries of care
plans and results are visible to patients by default. This ensures
patients are well-informed without the risk of uncontextualised
clinical notes causing unnecessary distress.
What happens to the patient
record if our contract with
MediLifecycle ends?

You own your data. In the event of contract termination,
MediLifecycle will provide a complete export of all patient records
in HL7 FHIR R4 format and all uploaded documents in their
original file formats within 30 days of termination. Data is retained
by MediLifecycle for 90 days post-termination to allow for dispute
resolution, then permanently deleted.
Can we use MediLifecycle if
we do not have a modern
EHR?

Yes. While EHR integration significantly enhances the value of
MediLifecycle, the platform functions as a standalone system. You
can register patients directly in MediLifecycle and use it as your
primary care coordination and patient communication platform.
Legacy EHR data can be imported via bulk CSV upload or manual
entry.
How does the AI know the
difference between a family
member asking an innocent
question and one who should
not have access?

The AI's responses are always bounded by the patient's
permission settings — it never reveals information beyond what
the patient has granted. If a family member asks about something
outside their permission scope, the AI responds with an
empathetic explanation and redirects them appropriately. It does
not confirm or deny the existence of withheld information.
Is the AI making clinical
decisions?

No. The AI provides clinical decision support — it surfaces
information, drafts documents, generates risk scores, and makes
suggestions. All clinical decisions remain the responsibility of the
licensed physician. All AI-generated clinical documents require
physician approval before being acted upon or delivered. The AI
cannot place orders, prescribe medications, or change a patient's
care plan without explicit physician authorisation.
What happens if a patient is
admitted to a hospital that is
not a MediLifecycle partner?

The patient's MediLifecycle record remains active and accessible
to their registered care team. The non-partner hospital cannot
access MediLifecycle directly, but the patient's primary doctor (if
they are a MediLifecycle user) can view the patient's full history
and communicate with the care team at the non-partner facility via
standard channels. The patient can continue to upload documents
from the non-partner facility.
Can we configure the platform
to match our specific clinical
governance structure?

Yes. Role-based access control is fully configurable. Approval
workflows, escalation chains, notification routing, AI approval
requirements, and lifecycle stage definitions are all configurable
by your Administrator. The Implementation Manager and Clinical
Workflows Specialist will help you map your governance structure
to the platform configuration during implementation.
How is the AI trained? Does it
learn from our patients' data?

The MediLifecycle AI engine is trained on a large, curated,
de-identified clinical dataset. It does not learn from individual
patients' data in a way that could cause that patient's information
to influence responses for other patients. Within a single patient's
record, the AI builds a longitudinal understanding of that patient's
specific patterns and history — this is scoped to that patient's
record only and cannot be accessed by any other patient's AI
context.
What is the mobile app
experience for patients?

The MediLifecycle patient mobile app (iOS and Android) mirrors
the web portal experience. It supports push notifications,
document upload from the device camera or file storage, secure
messaging, medication reminders, and AI assistant queries. The
app is available in 14 languages and includes an accessibility
mode. The app does not store PHI locally — all data is retrieved
from the platform on demand.
Can two hospitals within the
same health system share a
MediLifecycle deployment?

Yes. Multi-site Enterprise deployments support a single tenant
spanning multiple hospital sites with site-specific configuration,
reporting, and user management. Patients can be shared across
sites (subject to patient consent), and the clinical record follows
the patient across all sites in the network.

Glossary of Terms
Definitions for All Platform and Clinical Terms Used in This Guide
Term Definition

Active Patient A patient who has had at least one documented clinical interaction on the
MediLifecycle platform in the current billing month. Used as the basis for
per-patient pricing.

AI Alert Feed The AI's proactive notification stream — clinical alerts, overdue item
reminders, and risk score changes generated by the AI without being
prompted by a user query.

AI Confidence
Score

A percentage score displayed alongside AI diagnostic suggestions and risk
assessments, indicating the strength of the supporting evidence in the
patient's record. Not a probability of clinical outcome.
Audit Trail The immutable, chronological log of every user action, API call, AI query, AI
response, and data access event in the platform. Retained for 7 years.

BAA (Business
Associate
Agreement)

A HIPAA-required contract between a covered entity (hospital/clinic) and a
business associate (MediLifecycle) that governs the protection and use of
PHI. Signed at onboarding.
Care Gap A screening, preventive care measure, or clinical monitoring checkpoint that
is overdue for a patient based on their age, diagnosis, and applicable clinical
guidelines.

Care Plan A documented, structured plan agreed by the primary physician and patient
that specifies the diagnosis, treatment objectives, treatment modalities,
monitoring schedule, and discharge criteria for an episode of care.

DICOM Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine. The international standard
for medical imaging data format and transmission, used for X-rays, CT scans,
MRIs, and other imaging modalities.

DME (Durable
Medical Equipment)

Medical equipment prescribed for home use by a patient (e.g., wheelchairs,
CPAP machines, hospital beds, walkers). DME orders require a Certificate of
Medical Necessity (CMN) and payer authorisation.
FHIR (Fast
Healthcare
Interoperability
Resources)

A modern HL7 standard for exchanging healthcare information electronically.
FHIR R4 is the current version and the primary integration standard used by
MediLifecycle.
HL7 (Health Level
7)

A set of international standards for transfer of clinical and administrative data
between software applications. MediLifecycle supports both HL7 v2 (legacy)
and HL7 FHIR R4 (modern).
ICD-10 The 10th revision of the International Classification of Diseases. The
standard coding system for diagnoses and clinical conditions used in clinical
documentation and billing.

Lifecycle Stage One of the ten defined phases of a patient's care journey on the
MediLifecycle platform, from Registration to Post-Discharge Monitoring &
Closure. Stage transitions are controlled by the primary physician.

MDM (Master Data
Management)

In the context of mobile device management: the enterprise software used to
manage mobile devices issued to clinical staff. In the context of HL7: Medical
Document Management messages (MDM) used to exchange documents
between systems.
MFA (Multi-Factor
Authentication)

A security mechanism requiring users to provide two or more verification
factors to access the platform. Mandatory for all MediLifecycle users.
NEWS2 (National
Early Warning
Score 2)

A validated clinical scoring system used to assess the degree of illness of a
patient and prompt critical care response. MediLifecycle monitors NEWS2
scores for inpatient patients and alerts the care team when thresholds are
breached.
PA (Prior
Authorisation)

A payer requirement that certain medications, procedures, or investigations
must be pre-approved before the payer will cover the cost. MediLifecycle
automates PA submission and tracking.
PDMP (Prescription
Drug Monitoring
Program)

A state-level electronic database that tracks prescriptions for controlled
substances. MediLifecycle integrates with state PDMPs to support safe
controlled substance prescribing.
PHI (Protected
Health Information)

Any individually identifiable health information covered by HIPAA.
MediLifecycle treats all patient data as PHI and applies full HIPAA protections
accordingly.
RBAC (Role-Based
Access Control)

A security model that grants system access based on a user's defined role.
MediLifecycle's RBAC system ensures every user can only access data they
are explicitly authorised to see.
RPO / RTO Recovery Point Objective (RPO): the maximum acceptable data loss in a
disaster scenario (MediLifecycle: 1 hour). Recovery Time Objective (RTO):
the maximum acceptable time to restore full service after a disaster
(MediLifecycle: 4 hours).

SMART on FHIR A standard framework for authorising clinical applications to access EHR
data via FHIR APIs, maintaining clinical context (current patient, current
encounter) across applications.

SOC 2 Type II A third-party audit report demonstrating that a SaaS platform has maintained
effective security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy
controls over a period of time (typically 12 months).

Webhook A real-time data notification mechanism by which MediLifecycle sends an
HTTP POST to a configured endpoint whenever a defined event occurs on
the platform (e.g., new result received, stage changed).
